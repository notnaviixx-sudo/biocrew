Executive Summary

Food loss and waste are major global crises. Approximately 1.3 billion tons—one-third of all food produced—ends up wasted. This represents a massive waste of the water, land, energy, and labor used in its production. In Saudi Arabia, food waste costs the economy SAR 40 billion every year. Our project introduces anaerobic digestion as a sustainable alternative to convert scraps into biogas and nutrient-rich fertilizers.

The Challenge

When food waste is dumped into landfills, it is buried under tons of trash, creating an oxygen-free (anaerobic) environment. This causes it to decompose and release methane—a greenhouse gas much more potent than CO2. This process contributes to 8-10% of global greenhouse gas emissions.

Our Solution: Anaerobic Digestion

We transform consumer leftovers into valuable resources through a circular loop:

  • Biogas: A renewable energy source to power ovens, cars, and household appliances.
  • Organic Fertilizer: A nutrient-rich digestate (Nitrogen, Phosphorus, Potassium) that restores soil health and increases agricultural productivity.
  • Sustainability: Reduces landfill pollution and decreases reliance on fossil fuels.

Social & Environmental Impact

Our solution promotes environmental wellness by stopping methane release and preventing groundwater contamination from chemical fertilizers. It also supports social wellness by improving community hygiene and creating new job opportunities in waste collection and processing.

FAQ: Project Insights

What is the difference between food loss and food waste?

Food loss occurs early in the supply chain (harvest and transport). Food waste occurs at the retail and consumer level due to over-purchasing or improper storage.

How does this project help the local community?

By providing clean cooking fuel (biogas), we can significantly reduce exposure to harmful smoke and lower the risk of diseases associated with waste contamination.

Are there any risks with this technology?

Contamination from non-organic waste like plastic or metal can lower fertilizer quality. This is resolved through proper waste sorting and separation systems before processing.
